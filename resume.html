Wrote procedures for automating the loading of PC’s.


  • Wrote a set of table driven conversion utilities to allow data from old trading system to be migrated across to RingMaster.


  • Wrote the specification for a telex and fax system for the shipping company. Sourced a suitable hardware and software combination and integrated it into our network, feeding all incoming and outgoing telexes through to printers, an archive log and into cc:Mail for the users.


  • Due to the increased size of the company and the increasing reliance on IT, I felt it prudent to devise a strategy to try and remove the risks associated with the failure of any single piece of critical equipment. I migrated the server to Netware 4.10 SFTIII.

  • Installed a network in our New York office, similar to that in London and Geneva.

It found to be necessary to share some of the data in real-time, so I planned and implemented a Frame-Relay based WAN linking London, Geneva, New York and Pittsburgh with all of the network infrastructure centrally managed from London.


  • Due to the increased size of the company in London (about 120 active users), we introduced two more servers to handle the extra data being generate. These used overnight data mirroring to provide an extra level of redundancy.


  • Integrated a Linux server onto the network so that the remaining data from the previous trading software could be made available to some applications, and hosted on the Netware servers.


  • Due to the position of the building (on the corner or Old Street and City Road), it was felt that there was a risk of disruption to the business should a major incident happen. All the telecoms had been handled by C&W. I took over control of telecoms and negotiated with Worldcom for the supply of a fibre to be brought into the building at a point opposite the C&W one. Moving traffic across to Worldcom (and later Frontier as well) also reduced our call costs by 50%.


  • Following on from this I wrote a proposal for disaster recovery utilising an off-site server to mirror data overnight.


  • Apart from laptops, we deliberately kept all users running W3.11 in order to keep a standardised software configuration. I proposed that we switch over to NT to alleviate the deficiencies in 3.11. During the time that it took to get the proposal accepted, NT had moved from 3.51 to 4.


  • The original plan that I wrote for the NT workstation migration envisaged upgrading the memory & disk in most of the existing machines, however a combination of falling hardware prices and the low bundled price of NT4 with a new machine made it cost effective to replace the base units of all the machines. The plan called for users to go off in groups of 24 for migration training, at which point there machines were replaced with boxes preloaded with our standard NT4 build. Throughout the network all applications (currently numbering about 100) are distributed using Novell’s Zenworks. Before the migration this was a manual process dealt with by the helpdesk requiring a large amount of work. The project was completed on time and below budget.


  • Introduced network scanners for general purpose scanning (non office originated documents that have to go to other offices can be routed directly to a distant printer) and input to the fax servers, allowing least cost routing on outbound faxes.

This involved myself running a team of people to modify our software to the requirements of the Commission, and then port it on to all of their supported platforms. Porting the software consumed varying amounts of my time over the six years. Because of this work I have gained a good working knowledge of many different machines and their operating systems.

Because of our involvement with the CCTA's MITSY project I was working very closely with AT&T's Federal Systems Division from very early on in their development cycle for System V/MLS (A B2 secure UNIX). The CCTA ultimately decided not to proceed with Systems V/MLS, but before that point had been reached all of the necessary design and development work had been completed on our product to run in this environment.

As a company we were frequently asked by manufacturers to help them with their bids for major projects, these would involve us working very closely with the manufacturers to tune their machines to run our software as efficiently as possible.

EUROPEAN SPACE AGENCY
Software designer/programmer

I was employed on a fixed term contract to the European Space Agency. This involved the design of a new satellite control front end systems for the satellite controllers. Up until this time every controller had a glass tty and a Tektronix' terminal. This imposed a heavy workload on the two main Gould minicomputers so it was decided to replace these with a system containing an embedded processor, between 1 and 3 displays (any combination of graphics and alphanumeric displays), keyboard and an inkjet printer (the first one on the market.) This was built around an 8086 CPU card. I was responsible for the majority of the software design and implementation on this system which then performed all of the front end processing for this mini's. The project was very time critical in that the hardware had to be installed and running before the satellite Giotto was launched. This in turn was time critical in that it had to intercept Halley's comet.
